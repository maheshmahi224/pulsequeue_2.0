Blood pressure guidelines:
- Normal: systolic < 120, diastolic < 80
- Elevated: systolic 120-129, diastolic < 80  
- Stage 1 hypertension: systolic 130-139, diastolic 80-89
- Stage 2 hypertension: systolic >= 140, diastolic >= 90
- Hypertensive crisis: systolic > 180 or diastolic > 120
- Hypertensive crisis requires immediate medical attention

Pulse oximetry (SpO2) clinical guidelines:
- Normal: 95-100%
- Acceptable: 90-95%
- Concerning: 88-90% requires supplemental oxygen
- Critical: below 88% requires immediate intervention
- Oxygen delivery should begin when SpO2 drops below 94%

Blood glucose reference ranges:
- Fasting normal: 70-99 mg/dL
- Postprandial normal (2hr): below 140 mg/dL
- Prediabetes fasting: 100-125 mg/dL
- Diabetes fasting: above 126 mg/dL
- Diabetic emergency hypoglycemia: below 70 mg/dL (requires immediate glucose)
- Hyperglycemic crisis: above 400 mg/dL (DKA or HHS)
- Severe hypoglycemia: below 54 mg/dL (IV dextrose required)

Emergency triage protocols:
- Chest pain: Always immediate assessment, ECG within 10 minutes
- STEMI protocol: door-to-balloon time under 90 minutes
- Stroke protocol: CT scan within 25 minutes, thrombolytics within 4.5 hours of onset
- Respiratory distress: Immediate oxygen, monitor SpO2 continuously
- Anaphylaxis: Epinephrine IM immediately, IV access
- Sepsis: Lactate, blood cultures, antibiotics within 1 hour, fluids

Cardiac risk factors:
- Age over 65 significantly increases cardiovascular risk
- Hypertension doubles cardiovascular event risk
- Diabetes increases cardiovascular risk 2-4 fold
- History of MI increases risk of recurrent events by 20%
- Smoking, obesity, hyperlipidemia are modifiable risk factors
- Male gender and family history are non-modifiable risk factors

Respiratory emergency indicators:
- Breathing rate over 30/min indicates severe respiratory distress
- Accessory muscle use suggests significant respiratory compromise
- Cyanosis is a late sign of hypoxia
- Asthma attack: peak flow below 50% predicted is severe
- COPD exacerbation: increased work of breathing, worsening hypoxemia
- Pneumonia: fever, productive cough, decreased breath sounds

Neurological emergency signs:
- FAST: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call emergency
- Sudden severe headache (thunderclap) suggests subarachnoid hemorrhage
- Altered consciousness requires immediate assessment
- Seizures: protect airway, position safely, time the event
- TIA symptoms resolve within 24 hours but indicate high stroke risk

Diabetic emergencies:
- Hypoglycemia: shakiness, confusion, sweating, heart palpitations
- DKA: fruity breath, abdominal pain, vomiting, Kussmaul breathing
- HHS: extreme thirst, frequent urination, confusion, no ketones
- DKA treatment: fluid replacement, insulin, electrolyte monitoring
- HHS: gradual fluid replacement, insulin carefully titrated
